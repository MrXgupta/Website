Overview


Earth's Moon is the brightest and largest object in our night sky. The Moon makes Earth a more livable planet by moderating our home planet's wobble on its axis, leading to a relatively stable climate. It also causes tides, creating a rhythm that has guided humans for thousands of years.

The Moon was likely formed after a Mars-sized body collided with Earth several billion years ago.

Earth's only natural satellite is simply called "the Moon" because people didn't know other moons existed until Galileo Galilei discovered four moons orbiting Jupiter in 1610. In Latin, the Moon was called Luna, which is the main adjective for all things Moon-related: lunar.

Full Moon


The full moon, resplendent and glowing with its complete, circular brilliance, casts a serene and almost magical light across the night sky. Its luminous presence has long inspired awe and wonder, often linked to myths, legends, and romantic notions. As it bathes the world in its soft, silver radiance, the full moon highlights the beauty of the night, making the dark sky seem more inviting and alive with possibilities…

Half Moon


The half moon, visible during the first and last quarters of the lunar cycle, presents a striking contrast to its full counterpart. Its half-illuminated visage reveals a balance of light and shadow, offering a more subtle yet intriguing celestial display. This phase symbolizes transition and the passage of time, as it gradually shifts from waxing to waning, reflecting the natural rhythm of change and renewal in the lunar cycle.
